How to Read This System

This document is a design specification for Log, a thought capture application. Before diving into components and color values, you need to understand what Log is and why it looks the way it does.

Log is a Recording Instrument

Not a writing tool. Not a notes app. Not a journaling app. Log is a recording instrument in the lineage of seismographs, barographs, and ticker tapes. Instruments that capture faithfully, timestamp automatically, and never editorialize.

Every Element Serves the Core Purpose

Capture quickly. Archive permanently. Every design decision—from the period mark to the monospace timestamps to the idle trace—reinforces this purpose. If a design element doesn't help you capture or doesn't honor the permanence of the archive, it doesn't belong.

The System is Structural, Not Decorative

The two typefaces teach you the instrument's voice (monospace) versus your recorded words (proportional). The color system is silent until the instrument signals. The mark is made from the product's own materials. Form follows philosophy.

Reading the Visual Language

Throughout this specification, you'll see two distinct typographic voices:

  • MONOSPACE IBM Plex Mono — The mechanism's voice. Timestamps, labels, controls, metadata. Anything that belongs to the instrument itself.
  • Proportional IBM Plex Sans — The tape. Your words, what was recorded. The artifact.

When you see amber (#C4652A), the instrument is signaling. When you see archive purple (#5B5168), the archivist is annotating in the margins. Everything else is the warm-neutral paper scale.

Understanding the Philosophy

Log is not a writing tool. It's a recording instrument. This distinction grounds every design decision, from the mark to the color system to the behavior of the timeline.

The Period, Not the Cursor

A cursor says: waiting for input, potential, the blank page ahead. A period says: thought completed, entry committed, the record is made. Log isn't about the moment before you write. It's about the moment after. You captured a thought. It's in the log. Full stop.

The Archivist Principle

Your words are the artifact. The AI is the archivist. The instrument records faithfully—no interpretation, no improvement, no interference. The archivist reads what was written and annotates in the margins. This hierarchy is structural, not decorative.

Mechanism and Tape

The instrument has two materials: monospace for the mechanism (timestamps, labels, controls), proportional sans for the tape (your words, what was recorded). When you see monospace, you're looking at the instrument's voice. When you see proportional text, you're looking at what was captured.

Recording Instrument Lineage

Log descends from instruments that share a pattern: append-only, faithful capture, pen-and-drum mechanism. They don't interpret. They don't improve. They record.

  • 132 CE Zhang Heng's Seismoscope — First known recording instrument. Detects and marks events without human intervention.
  • 1760s Cumming's Barograph — Continuous trace on rotating drum. The pen never lifts. The instrument records whether anyone is watching.
  • 1867 Calahan's Stock Ticker — Append-only paper tape. Each entry timestamped by position. The ticker doesn't editorialize.
  • 1871 Edison's Universal Ticker — Standardized the ticker tape format. Fidelity over interpretation.
  • 1881 Ewing's Seismogram — Needle on smoked drum. The dot at the start of a deflection is the seismogram's "period" — the mark that says "something was recorded here."

The Seven Brand Principles

These are constraints, not aspirations. They govern every design decision.

01

The brand is the surface, not a symbol applied to it.

Log's identity lives in the type system, the color tokens, the materiality, the rhythm of the timeline. The mark is made from the same materials as the product. There is no separation between "brand design" and "product design" — they are one.

02

The period, not the cursor.

Permanence, not potential. A thought was captured. The record is made. Full stop. This positions Log as fundamentally different from writing tools that center the blank page, the blinking cursor, the moment before you begin.

03

Monochrome until it means something.

Color is signal, never decoration. The neutral paper scale carries all structure and hierarchy. Color enters the UI only when the instrument is signaling — intent classification, the amber commit button, the archivist's annotation layer. If a color doesn't carry semantic meaning, it doesn't belong.

04

Stillness over performance.

The mark doesn't animate. The timeline doesn't shift. AI doesn't announce itself. Trust is built through reliability, not spectacle. The instrument is patient, constant, honest. No loading spinners, no celebration animations, no "processing..." messages. The work happens quietly.

05

The mechanism and the tape.

Monospace is the mechanism (IBM Plex Mono). Proportional sans is the tape (IBM Plex Sans). Two materials, two roles. The distinction is structural — it teaches users the philosophy through the typography itself. The instrument speaks in monospace. Your words appear in proportional text.

06

The drum turns between entries.

The instrument doesn't sleep. Real recording instruments don't stop when you walk away — a barograph keeps tracing pressure, a seismograph keeps turning its drum. The idle trace marks time honestly. It's the only embellishment in the system, and it earns its place by being true to the instrument metaphor.

07

The tape gets wider, but the instrument stays the same.

Voice, camera, files — these are new input modalities. Recipes, calendar events, contacts — these are new output interpretations. But the commit button, the timeline spine, and the append-only record all stay constant. New pens on the same drum. The instrument expands its range without losing its identity.

iA Writer owns the cursor as a brand mark. Their entire identity is built around it — their logo is a cursor, their app icon is a cursor. Adopting a cursor mark would position Log as a writing tool (which it isn't) and create a direct collision with an established, respected product.

The cursor and the period are antonyms. The cursor says: waiting for input, potential, the blank page ahead. The period says: thought completed, entry committed, the record is made. Log centers the moment after — you captured a thought, it's in the record, full stop.

Motion Specification

Philosophy: Stillness over performance. Log uses motion sparingly. Animation appears only when it serves the instrument metaphor or provides essential feedback. No celebration, no spectacle, just honest mechanical behavior.

Core Motion Principles

1. The mark doesn't animate. Logo and wordmark are always static.
2. The timeline doesn't shift. Entries appear instantly, no slide-in effects.
3. AI doesn't announce itself. No "thinking..." spinners or typing indicators.
4. Mechanical honesty. Button presses feel like physical mechanisms. The commit button depresses like a shutter release.

Motion Budget

6 animations total: Blinking caret, intent dot pulse, commit button press, entry slide-up, idle trace growth, dark mode transition, color copy feedback.

0 celebration animations. No confetti, no success checkmarks, no "great job!" messages. The instrument doesn't congratulate you for using it. It just records faithfully.

The Idle Trace

Philosophical Grounding: Real recording instruments don't stop when you walk away. A barograph keeps tracing atmospheric pressure. A seismograph keeps turning its drum. When you come back, the gap between marks tells you how long you were away. The instrument was running.

This is Log's only embellishment — and it earns its place by being true to the instrument metaphor. No streak guilt, no "Welcome back!" messages, no color change as the gap grows. Just the honest record of silence.

Design Rules

  • Visual properties: 1px hairline, Paper-20 color, left-spine aligned
  • Height algorithm: Logarithmic scaling (not linear) — the visual difference between 1 hour and 6 hours is larger than between 2 weeks and 3 weeks
  • 80pt ceiling: Hard ceiling. Whether you've been away one week or six months, your last entry is never more than one comfortable scroll from the compose area
  • No animation: Always present as if it was always there. Stillness over performance
  • Label format: Monospace, specific ("2 days," not "2d"), only for gaps >24 hours

Entry Append: The Mechanical Cycle

When the user taps commit, a three-stage mechanical cycle makes the entry feel physically inscribed into the log, not just appearing in a list.

The Mechanical Metaphor

A ticker tape machine works in stages: the key is pressed (shutter), the tape advances (mechanism moves), the mark appears (inscription), the paper settles (ready state). Log's entry append mirrors this exact mechanical sequence.

Why Each Element Exists

Nothing in Log is decorative. Every element serves the core purpose: capture quickly, archive permanently. This section explains the reasoning behind each design decision.

Period Mark (•)
Signals permanence and completion. Unlike a cursor (which says "waiting for input"), the period says "the record is made." It's the instrument's signature—the mark it leaves on every entry. At small sizes, the period becomes the dominant visual element, which is correct: the instrument's job is to record, and the period is proof that it did.
Intent Classification
Helps you understand your thinking patterns over time. Thought, Action, Decision, Reference—these aren't productivity categories, they're semantic patterns. The archivist classifies asynchronously, never interrupting capture. The 6px dots provide at-a-glance pattern recognition when scrolling the timeline.
Monospace vs. Proportional
Separates the instrument's voice from your content. Monospace (IBM Plex Mono) is the mechanism: timestamps, labels, controls, metadata. Proportional (IBM Plex Sans) is the tape: your words, what was recorded. This distinction teaches the system's philosophy through typography itself.
Idle Trace
Honors the passing of time honestly. Real recording instruments don't stop when you walk away—a seismograph keeps turning its drum, a barograph keeps tracing pressure. The idle trace is the gap in the record. It tells you the instrument was running. No guilt, no "welcome back," just the honest record of silence.
Warm-Neutral Paper Scale
Creates a calm, archival feel without being stark. Not gray (too digital), not white (too harsh). The warm cast suggests aged paper, instrument panels, microfilm readers. Dark mode inverts the scale while keeping signal colors constant—the structure remains, only the luminance changes.
Amber Signal Color
The instrument's only voice. When you see amber, the instrument is signaling: the period mark, the commit button loaded with text, the active caret. Amber is warmer than red (not urgent), more assertive than yellow (not cautious). It says "ready to record."
Archive Purple (Archivist)
The archivist's color. Muted, scholarly, always in the margins. When you see #5B5168, you're seeing the archivist's annotations—never the user's words. This maintains the hierarchy: your words are the artifact, the AI is the archivist.
80pt Idle Trace Ceiling
Prevents the timeline from becoming unwieldy. Whether you've been away one week or six months, your last entry is never more than one comfortable scroll from the compose area. The ceiling is about usability, not punishment. No streak guilt.
Append-Only Timeline
Entries can't be edited or deleted in MVP. This isn't a limitation—it's the instrument's core behavior. Recording instruments don't have an undo button. The tape is the tape. Post-MVP may add archiving or annotation, but the original entry remains immutable.
No Streaks or Gamification
Log doesn't reward you for using it daily. The idle trace marks time honestly, without judgment. The instrument doesn't care if you use it every day or once a month. It just records faithfully when you do.

Using the System

This section demonstrates how to apply the design system when building new features or components.

Example Use Case: Adding a Search Feature

Step 1: Determine the Voice

Is this element part of the mechanism or the tape?

  • Search input placeholder: Mechanism voice → Monospace, "Search entries..."
  • Search results text: Tape voice → Proportional (your recorded words)
  • Result count: Mechanism voice → Monospace, "24 entries found"

Step 2: Determine Color Usage

Ask: Is the instrument signaling, or is this structure/hierarchy?

  • Search icon: Paper-40 (structural element, not a signal)
  • Highlighted search terms: Amber background (instrument signaling "match found")
  • Result separators: Paper-10 (structural hierarchy)

Step 3: Apply Spacing

Use 8px baseline grid. Never arbitrary values.

  • Search input padding: var(--s-12) (12px)
  • Between results: var(--s-24) (24px)
  • Section margin: var(--s-48) (48px)

System Boundaries

MVP Scope

In scope: Text entry, intent classification, timeline view, idle trace, commit button, dark mode, search/filter by intent.

Post-MVP: Voice recording, image capture, rich entry types (recipe, calendar), archivist annotations, export formats.

Out of scope: Real-time collaboration, social features, public sharing, streaks/gamification, entry editing (append-only is core philosophy).

Keyboard Navigation Support

  • All interactive elements are keyboard accessible via Tab/Shift+Tab
  • Focus indicators always visible (2px amber outline)
  • Logical tab order follows visual layout
  • No keyboard traps—users can always navigate away
  • Skip links provided for screen readers

Screen Reader Considerations

  • All images have descriptive alt text
  • Intent dots use role="img" with aria-label
  • Loading states announced with aria-live="polite"
  • Form errors linked to inputs with aria-describedby
  • Landmark regions (main, nav, article) clearly defined

Reduced Motion Support

Users who enable "prefers-reduced-motion" get a static interface. All animations are disabled, including the blinking caret, pulsing dots, and transitions.

@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
    .blinking-caret,
    .loading-dot,
    .animated-trace {
        animation: none;
    }

    * {
        transition: none !important;
    }

    /* Caret becomes static */
    .blinking-caret {
        opacity: 1;
    }
}

Glossary of Terms

Key concepts and terminology used throughout the Log design system.

Intent
The semantic classification of an entry. Thought, Action, Decision, Reference, or Unknown. Classified asynchronously by the archivist, visualized with colored dots on the timeline.
Mechanism
The instrument's own voice. Timestamps, labels, controls, metadata—anything rendered in IBM Plex Mono. Represents the recording apparatus itself.
Tape
What was recorded. User-created content rendered in IBM Plex Sans. The artifact, the record, your words. Always proportional typography.
Archivist
The AI's role in Log. Not an assistant, not a collaborator—an archivist. Classifies entries by intent, annotates in the margins, prepares structured outputs. Never interrupts capture.
Recording Instrument
The foundational metaphor. Log is not a writing tool—it's a recording instrument in the lineage of seismographs, barographs, ticker tapes. Instruments that capture faithfully and never editorialize.
Idle Trace
The visual gap on the timeline between entries. Height grows logarithmically based on time elapsed. Represents the drum turning while you were away.
Paper Tones
The 12-step warm-neutral color scale (Paper-00 through Paper-95). Not gray—has a warm cast like aged paper or darkened instrument panels. Carries all structure and hierarchy.
Signal Colors
The five intent classification colors: Thought (#6B7B8D), Action (#C4652A), Decision (#8B6E4E), Reference (#5B7A6B), Unknown (#928A7E). Only colors used semantically beyond the paper scale.
Commit
The act of permanently recording an entry. Triggered by clicking the commit button or pressing Cmd/Ctrl+Enter. Once committed, entries are immutable (in MVP).
Append-Only
Core architectural principle. Entries are added to the timeline but never edited or deleted. The record is permanent. Recording instruments don't have undo.
Period Mark
The amber dot (•) in the Log mark. Represents permanence, completion, "the record is made." Antonym of the cursor (which represents potential).
The Drum
Metaphorical reference to the rotating drum in mechanical recording instruments (seismographs, barographs). In Log, "the drum turns" between entries—time passes, the idle trace grows.

Beyond Text

Post-MVP, Log expands to capture voice, images, and files. The principle: "The tape gets wider, but the instrument stays the same."

Voice Recording

Microphone icon appears beside commit button. Compose area becomes live amber waveform. Archivist transcribes afterward; the audio is the artifact. The waveform is the mark on the tape — not decoration, but the shape of what was recorded.

Rich Entries

When text entries contain structured data (recipe, calendar event, contact), the detail view surfaces a structured preview. The user's raw capture is always the artifact. The structured output is subordinate — prepared by the "clerk," not the archivist.

The Governing Principle

New input modalities (voice, camera, files) are new pens on the same drum. The commit button, timeline spine, and append-only record remain constant. The instrument expands its range without losing its identity.

Decision Log Highlights

Key design decisions and the reasoning behind them. Why these choices, not others.

Why Period, Not Cursor

Decision: The amber period (•) as the brand mark, not a cursor.

Context: iA Writer owns the cursor as a brand element. Their logo is a cursor, their app icon is a cursor, their identity is built around the writing moment.

Rationale: The cursor and period are antonyms. Cursor = waiting for input, potential, the blank page. Period = thought completed, entry committed, the record is made. Log is fundamentally about the moment after capture, not before. The period positions Log correctly in the market and avoids direct collision with an established product.

Why IBM Plex (Not SF Fonts)

Decision: IBM Plex Mono and IBM Plex Sans as the type system.

Alternatives considered: SF Mono + SF Pro (native Apple fonts), Inconsolata + Inter, Monaco + system sans.

Rationale: IBM Plex Mono has warmer, more intentional letterforms than SF Mono. It feels like an instrument's typeface, not a code editor's. IBM Plex Sans has excellent readability and warmth for body text. Both are freely available, cross-platform, and designed as a unified system. SF fonts would be more "native" on iOS but lose personality and lock us into Apple platforms.

Trade-off acknowledged: Less native feel on iOS, but gains character, warmth, and platform independence.

Why These Exact Colors

Decision: 12-step warm-neutral paper scale, 5 signal colors, 2 brand accents.

Color selection criteria:

  • Paper tones must have warm cast (not pure gray)
  • All signal colors tested for WCAG AA contrast on Paper-00 and Paper-95
  • Signal colors must feel semantic, not decorative
  • Amber (#C4652A) chosen as primary because it's warm, functional, and distinct
  • Archive purple (#5B5168) chosen for scholarly, marginal quality

Rationale: The paper scale supports the instrument metaphor—aged paper, darkened panels. Signal colors only appear when they mean something (intent classification, archivist notes, instrument signals). No decoration.

Why Idle Trace (Not Streaks)

Decision: Logarithmic idle trace on timeline, not streak counting.

Alternatives considered: Streak counter, "X days logged" badge, calendar heatmap, no visualization.

Rationale: Real instruments don't stop when you walk away. A barograph keeps tracing pressure whether you're watching or not. The idle trace is honest—it shows you were away, but doesn't punish you for it. Logarithmic scaling matches how absence feels: 2 hours vs 8 hours matters, but 8 hours vs 24 hours feels similar (both "a day"). No guilt mechanics, no gamification.

Implementation detail: Height = 24px × log₂(minutes / 30). Caps at 80px for readability.

Why Append-Only (No Edit, No Delete)

Decision: Entries are immutable once committed. No editing, no deletion in MVP.

Rationale: Recording instruments don't have undo. A seismograph doesn't let you erase yesterday's earthquake. A ticker tape doesn't let you revise stock prices. The permanence is part of the value—your log is a faithful record of what you thought when you thought it. Edit/delete features can come post-MVP as "corrections" or "annotations," but the original entry always remains.

User benefit: No decision paralysis, no perfectionism, no shame about changing your mind. Just capture.

Why Asynchronous Classification

Decision: Intent classification happens asynchronously by the archivist, not inline during typing.

Alternatives considered: Real-time classification, manual tagging, no classification.

Rationale: Capture must be instant and uninterrupted. Classification can wait. The archivist works in the background, thinks for 1-3 seconds, then annotates the entry. This maintains the instrument metaphor—the pen marks the tape immediately, the archivist catalogs it later. The user never waits, never tags manually, never has their flow broken.
